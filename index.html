Research and Internships

Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics

Unmasking Stellar-Feedback Driven Bubbles: Identification and Properties Analysis

The identification and tracking of stellar feedback-driven galaxy bubbles is an important topic in star formation and galactic structure research. However, current observational analysis of bubbles is limited in scope; information on bubble lifetime, temperature, and density is inaccessible. Simulation data thus provides a unique opportunity to glean some of these characteristics at high resolution. We present an investigation into the identification of hot, ionized bubbles in the interstellar medium and an analysis of some of their properties in a dwarf spiral (NGC300-like) galaxy. By explicitly calculating the average radius, lifetime, temperature, density, and spatial distribution of the identified bubbles using Lagrangian gas parcels, we constrain some of these properties. Additionally, we examine the relationship between these characteristics and the local environment of each bubble. Finally, we threshold the analysis to predict how the data would appear experimentally and compare to true observations.

This paper is near submission and will be published in the Astrophysical Journal. We presented our work at the Harvard-Heidelberg 2023 Star Formation Conference. We also recently presented a poster at the 243rd AAS meeting in New Orleans, LA. You can find the poster here.


E Ink Co-op

For my first co-op (6 month internship) during my time at Northeastern University, I worked in applied research at E Ink Corporation. My research focused on probing the fundamental electrophoretic behaviour of the ink in ePaper devices. ePaper devices work by applying electric fields to charged pigment particles in order to layer them on top of each other, reflecting the perfect amount of each wavelength of light to produce a full range of colors. I produced large sets of optical data which I then built analysis pipelines for, and I developed a variety of test sequences. In particular, I investigated temperature dependence of pigment mobility.


Sridhar Lab

During my second year at Northeastern, I conducted medical physics research at Northeastern's Sridhar Lab. The Sridhar Lab currently works on utilizing ferumoxtyl, a novel contrast agent, in MRI angiographs. Through a variety of grouping methods — growing from seeds, thresholding, simple manual editing, and more – I identified patients' kidneys, aortas, inferior vena cavas, and renal blood vessels in MRI signal data, including cysts in the patients’ kidneys that could be diagnosed. These 3D models were created for use as a machine learning training set.
